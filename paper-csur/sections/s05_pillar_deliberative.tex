\section{Pillar 2: Language as Deliberative Feedback}
\label{sec:deliberative}

With the task specified through a grounding signal (\S\ref{sec:grounding}), we turn to methods that refine behavior within an episode without updating model parameters, the regime closest to test-time compute scaling \citep{snell2024scaling}. The four cells of this pillar differ in where critique originates: the model itself or an external tool, peer models, past episodes, or parallel candidate paths. Following the assignment rule of \S\ref{sec:taxonomy}, a method belongs here when the linguistic feedback it produces is consumed in the current context window; when the same feedback is instead compressed into a dataset, a reward, or weights, the method moves to \S\ref{sec:learning}. We discuss self-generated and tool-generated critique together, since they share one loop structure and differ mainly in the origin and reliability of the feedback.

\subsection{Self-Critique and Externally Grounded Critique}
\label{subsec:critique}

The simplest deliberative loop asks the model to critique its own output and revise accordingly. Self-Refine \citep{madaan2023selfrefine} formalized the pattern, building on the demonstration in Constitutional AI \citep{bai2022constitutional} that models can evaluate outputs against written principles. The central obstacle is circularity: the critic and the generator are the same model and share the same blind spots. Replacing self-generated critique with evaluation from a separately trained critic improves reasoning \citep{paul2024refiner}, evidence that feedback quality, not the revision mechanism, is the bottleneck. Self-correction degrades when the critique source shares the generator's failure modes \citep{huang2023large, kamoi2024can}, and the degradation is sharpest in smaller models \citep{olausson2024self}. Mitigations include Socratic decomposition of the task into independently checkable steps \citep{shi2025ssr} and parallel refinement across several candidates \citep{wang2025learning}. None of these helps when the error stems from missing knowledge, which no amount of introspection can supply.

External grounding breaks the circle by anchoring critique in deterministic tool output \citep{schick2023toolformer}. Execution traces, unit-test verdicts, search results, and API responses \citep{chen2024teaching, gou2024toratoolintegratedreasoningagent} give the model verified evidence from which to generate corrected outputs \citep{gou2024critic, yang2024swe, wang2024executable}, and reinforcement learning can teach models to exploit such feedback more effectively \citep{gehring2024rlef}, an early instance of the migration toward \S\ref{sec:learning} that recurs throughout this pillar. Grounding shifts the dominant failure mode from blind spots to trust asymmetry: the tool output is accurate, but the model may misattribute the root cause behind a reported trace. The approach also inherits practical costs, including infrastructure overhead and latency, and it remains confined to domains that expose a verifiable oracle.

\subsection{Multi-Agent Debate}
\label{subsec:debate}

Multi-agent debate distributes critique across parallel model instances, often assigning distinct personas to widen perspective \citep{hong2024metagpt}. Exchanging critiques improves reasoning even among identical models \citep{du2023debate}, role diversity strengthens the discussion further \citep{chan2023chatevalbetterllmbasedevaluators}, models can be trained to revise their reasoning under conflicting opinions \citep{liu2026learningselfdebatepreparingreasoning}, and adaptive heterogeneous line-ups improve mathematical reasoning \citep{zhou2025adaptive}.

A second wave of work asks when this apparatus pays for itself. Reframed as a test-time scaling technique combining collaborative refinement with diverse exploration, debate offers limited advantage over single-agent scaling on mathematical reasoning but becomes more effective as problem difficulty rises and model capability falls; on safety tasks, collaborative refinement can increase vulnerability, while diverse agent configurations gradually reduce attack success \citep{yang2025revisiting}, a finding \S\ref{sec:safety} revisits. A systematic evaluation of five representative debate methods across nine benchmarks and four foundation models reaches a blunter verdict: debate often fails to outperform single-agent chain-of-thought and self-consistency despite much higher inference cost, and model heterogeneity is the one intervention that consistently helps \citep{zhang2025stop}. This matches the repeated observation that debaters sharing biases converge to redundant consensus \citep{estornell2024multi, liang2024encouragingdivergentthinkinglarge} and that current debate methods do not reliably beat simpler single-agent strategies \citep{choi2026debate}.

Communication structure is the other economic lever. Full-broadcast designs incur substantial token overhead \citep{choi2026debate}, motivating sparse topologies \citep{li2024improving}, per-round retention of only the responses that maximally disagree with one another and with the majority vote \citep{nguyen2026hear}, and explicit confidence expression, so that an agent holding a genuine knowledge advantage communicates it rather than capitulating to the group \citep{lin2025enhancing}.

Debate also functions as an oversight protocol in which a weaker judge arbitrates between stronger debaters. Debate lifts non-expert model judges to 76\% accuracy and human judges to 88\%, against naive baselines of 48\% and 60\% \citep{khan2024debating}, and it beats consultancy across tasks, outperforming direct question answering under information asymmetry, with mixed results without it \citep{kenton2024scalable}. Because the same mechanism recurs across pillars, we classify debate systems by where the transcript ends up: consumed in the context window (this pillar), distilled into training data \citep{motwani2024malt, subramaniam2025multiagent}, scored as a reward channel \citep{park2025maporl}, or internalized into a single model's weights \citep{yi2026latent}, all destinations surveyed in \S\ref{sec:learning}.

\subsection{Experiential Memory}
\label{subsec:memory}

Where the preceding cells regenerate feedback from scratch in every episode, experiential memory persists lessons for reuse. Dedicated surveys already map this territory as a component question, cataloguing episodic, semantic, and procedural stores together with their design and evaluation \citep{zhang2025survey, du2026memory}; our treatment is deliberately narrower and role-based. Memory enters VRL when the stored artifact is itself a linguistic feedback signal that a later episode consumes as deliberative context. This stance also resolves the boundary question that memory poses for any taxonomy: lessons persist across episodes, yet we classify by the moment of consumption, since a retrieved reflection conditions the current episode exactly as a fresh critique would. Work formalizing deployment-time learning as a lifecycle stage of its own, after pre-training and post-training, makes the same cut \citep{guo2026cascade}. A complementary boundary separates memory from language-space optimization, and \S\ref{sec:optimizers} states the deciding rule in full: in brief, a store the agent itself reads and writes during episodes acts as deliberative feedback and stays here, whereas a context curated by an outer loop against a task metric is a parameter of the system.

What should persist depends on the recurrence structure of the task. Raw reflections are cheapest and work when later episodes closely resemble the failure that produced them. Reflexion established this regime, raising HumanEval pass@1 to 91\% from a prior 80\% GPT-4 result \citep{shinn2023reflexion}. When experience must transfer across tasks, distilled principles dominate transcripts because they remove episode-specific detail: ExpeL contrasts successes and failures, MetaReflection compresses failed trials into portable instructions, and related systems induce reusable workflows or strategy notes \citep{zhao2024expel,gupta2024metareflection,wang2024agent,ouyang2025reasoningbank,suzgun2026dynamic}. Structured stores become worthwhile only when scale or collaboration makes flat retrieval the bottleneck. Interlinked notes, consolidation, team graphs, and reconstruction-time retrieval trade added memory management for lower context cost and more selective access \citep{xu2025amem,chhikara2025mem0,zhang2025gmemory,ji2026memory}. Finally, tasks that demand reliable re-execution favor procedural artifacts over prose. Code and instruction libraries can transfer skills, test them, and govern admission using attributed evidence, properties that free-form reflections cannot guarantee \citep{wang2023voyager,fang2025memp,zhang2026agentfactory,lin2026museautoskill,zhou2026mementoskills,yang2026autoskill,liu2026skillsvote}. Thus the dominant design moves from reflection to abstraction to executable procedure as recurrence weakens, store scale grows, and behavioral reproducibility becomes more important.

A second decision is who controls writing. Append-only stores suffice when episodes are few and stationary. Longer-lived agents need utility-based pruning to prevent stale lessons from dominating retrieval \citep{cao2025remember,zhang2026liveevo}. Learned write policies become attractive when add, update, delete, and no-op decisions repeat often enough to justify training; Memory-R1, AutoMem, and SkillRL optimize these operations or co-evolve the skill library with the policy \citep{yan2025memoryr1,wu2026automem,xia2026skillrl}. Meta-evolution goes further by searching the encode, store, retrieve, and manage pipeline itself, but pays a larger optimization and evaluation cost \citep{zhang2025memevolve,liu2026mempro,zhang2026memskill}. These trained writers do not change the taxonomy assignment. The stored text still affects behavior through the context window, while the scalar outcome reward used to train the writer contains no linguistic structure and therefore falls outside the definitional fence of \S\ref{sec:framework}.

Persistence cuts both ways. Poor memories propagate mistakes, growing stores demand increasingly complex retrieval \citep{park2023genagents}, stale knowledge misleads in non-stationary environments \citep{zhang2025survey}, and adversarially injected memories can cause cross-session drift \citep{dong2026memory}, a risk examined in \S\ref{sec:safety}. The sharpest counterweight is evidence that continuous consolidation itself corrupts: agents that preserve raw episodes double the accuracy of counterparts forced to consolidate after every interaction, which suggests that consolidation should be explicitly gated rather than habitual \citep{zhang2026useful}. Evaluation is meanwhile consolidating around benchmarks probing accurate retrieval, test-time learning, long-range understanding, and selective forgetting \citep{tan2025membench, hu2025evaluating, wei2025evomemory, belikova2026managing}, to which \S\ref{sec:evaluation} returns.

\subsection{Evaluator Feedback in Search}
\label{subsec:search}

The final cell covers deliberation that branches. Rather than refining a single trajectory, these methods explore multiple candidate paths and use verbal feedback to decide which to expand, prune, or abandon \citep{wang-etal-2025-dont}. The inclusion criterion is the verbal evaluator signal consumed during search, not the search procedure itself: decoding or sampling strategies that involve no linguistic feedback, such as majority voting over sampled chains, fall outside VRL. Tree of Thoughts extends chain-of-thought prompting \citep{wei2022chain} with tree search over intermediate thoughts scored by self-assessment \citep{yao2023tree}; Reasoning via Planning \citep{hao2023reasoning} and Graph of Thoughts \citep{besta2024graph} generalize the topology to non-linear dependencies such as aggregation and refinement. The distinctive advantage is that verbal evaluation applies at every node, so unproductive paths are abandoned early; the distinctive cost is that exploring many states multiplies model calls, making these methods far more expensive than single-pass generation \citep{xie2023self}.

\paragraph{Synthesis: when this pillar wins.}
Three variables organize the four cells. The first is feedback quality, the binding constraint everywhere: self-critique is the cheapest and least reliable source, debate buys reliability only from genuine diversity, and tool output is the most reliable signal but exists only where the domain exposes an oracle. The second is circularity, which grounding breaks, debate dilutes, and memory can quietly reintroduce by entrenching early errors. The third is compute: debate and search multiply model calls within an episode, whereas memory amortizes deliberation across episodes and can even invert the cost curve, as when a small model with a refined experience pool outperforms a larger memoryless one \citep{cao2025remember}. Deliberative feedback is therefore the pillar of choice when weights are frozen behind an API, when tasks recur so that lessons amortize, and when problems are hard relative to model capability. When training access exists and the task distribution is stationary, the same transcripts are usually better internalized as learning signals at a fraction of the inference cost (\S\ref{sec:learning}); \S\ref{sec:practitioner} turns these conditions into an explicit decision procedure.
