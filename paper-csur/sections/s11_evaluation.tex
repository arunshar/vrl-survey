\section{Evaluation and Benchmarks}
\label{sec:evaluation}

A survey of feedback mechanisms is incomplete without an account of how the field measures them. This section takes stock of the benchmark ecosystem that has grown around verbal reinforcement learning (VRL) and organizes it around a distinction that recurs throughout this survey: feedback \emph{quality}, whether a signal is accurate, calibrated, and unbiased, and feedback \emph{utilization}, whether the receiving model actually improves when the signal is supplied. The distinction matters because VRL inherited its evaluation habits from RLHF, where reward-model accuracy on held-out preference pairs stood in for signal quality and end-task performance stood in for everything else; the benchmark families reviewed here decompose that middle ground. The two axes are separable failure modes. A critique can be correct yet ignored, and a reward model can score well on an accuracy benchmark that barely correlates with the policies it later trains \citep{kim2024evaluating}. Benchmark coverage also maps onto the signal families developed in \S\ref{sec:taxonomy} and \S\ref{sec:rewardstack}: outcome preference, judge verdict, natural-language critique, process supervision, and interactive language feedback. Table~\ref{tab:benchmarks} indexes the major families by what they measure, the signal type they probe, and the stage at which that signal operates.

\subsection{Measuring Feedback Quality}
\label{subsec:eval-quality}

RewardBench \citep{lambert2024rewardbench} established the template for evaluating outcome-preference signals: prompt-chosen-rejected trios spanning chat, reasoning, and safety, scored on a public leaderboard, with findings on refusal propensity and reasoning limitations that frame reward-model evaluation as a window into which values RLHF embeds. Its successors harden the template. RewardBench~2 \citep{malik2025rewardbench} builds from new human prompts rather than prompts recycled from downstream evaluations; models score about 20 points lower on average while the benchmark remains highly correlated with best-of-$N$ selection and PPO training outcomes. RM-Bench \citep{liu2024rmbench} separates sensitivity to subtle content changes from susceptibility to style: state-of-the-art reward models average only 46.6\% under style-bias interference, below the 50\% random level. RewardMATH \citep{kim2024evaluating} shows that the math subset of the original benchmark, built on single chosen-rejected comparisons, shows almost no correlation with optimized-policy results, while its one-to-many redesign correlates strongly and can estimate reward overoptimization. RMB \citep{zhou2024rmb} widens coverage to more than 49 real-world scenarios with pairwise and best-of-$N$ protocols, and Preference Proxy Evaluations \citep{frick2024how} validate proxy metrics against an end-to-end RLHF experiment. The methodological throughline is blunt: an accuracy number on preference pairs means something only insofar as it predicts downstream utility. Personalized RewardBench \citep{ma2026personalized} extends the question to pluralistic alignment, where state-of-the-art reward models peak at 75.94\% accuracy on user-specific rubrics.

A parallel line meta-evaluates LLM judges, whose verdicts serve both as evaluation and as reward (\S\ref{sec:rewardstack}). LLMBar \citep{zeng2023evaluating} curates output pairs in which the deviating response has deceptive surface appeal, and finds, contrary to earlier meta-evaluations, that evaluators differ sharply and even the best leave substantial room for improvement. JudgeBench \citep{tan2024judgebench} ties preference labels to objective correctness and finds strong models such as GPT-4o performing only slightly better than random guessing. For VRL these are not merely evaluation concerns: because judge verdicts are recycled as rewards (\S\ref{sec:rewardstack}), measurement error becomes training signal, and a biased judge does not just misrank systems but steers policies. Reliability audits go further. CALM quantifies twelve judge biases through principle-guided input perturbation \citep{ye2024justice}; BiasScope discovers biases automatically and distills them into JudgeBench-Pro, on which even powerful evaluators show error rates above 50\% \citep{lai2026biasscope}; and Sage measures preference consistency without human labels, finding that frontier models fail to maintain consistent preferences in nearly a quarter of difficult cases \citep{feng2025are}. \citet{feuer2025when} audit judge-based leaderboards themselves, documenting schema incoherence and factor collapse and showing that Elo-style aggregation masks genuine ranking uncertainty. Coverage is also widening: MM-Eval finds that judges strong in English have considerable room for improvement on non-English text across 18 languages \citep{son2024mmeval}, IF-RewardBench moves from pairwise to listwise judging via preference graphs \citep{wen2026ifrewardbench}, and SCAN argues that evaluation should return fine-grained verbal capability reports rather than a single rank \citep{wang2025scan}.

Critique-quality benchmarks address the natural-language critiques at the center of the deliberative pillar (\S\ref{sec:deliberative}). CriticBench \citep{lin2024criticbench} evaluates generation, critique, and correction jointly across five reasoning domains, finding the three capabilities linearly related and observing that stronger models critique weaker ones better while weaker models can surpass stronger ones at self-critique. CriticEval adds human-annotated reference critiques so that textual critiques can be graded reliably \citep{lan2024criticeval}, and CodeCriticBench extends checklist-scored critique evaluation to code \citep{zhang2025codecriticbench}. At the step level, ProcessBench \citep{zheng2024processbench} matters here for its protocol: models must localize the earliest erroneous step in competition mathematics or declare the solution correct, a design that scores step-level judgment directly rather than through downstream accuracy and places process reward models and prompted critic models under one comparison, whose training-side implications \S\ref{sec:rewardstack} discusses. PRMBench probes finer error dimensions and uncovers significant weaknesses in fine-grained error detection \citep{song2025prmbench}, while ToolComp carries process supervision into multi-tool trajectories, where most models score below 50\% accuracy and PRMs outrank outcome reward models by 19\% and 11\% rank@1 when ranking base and fine-tuned trajectories \citep{nath2025toolcomp}. Verifier benchmarks close the loop with training: VerifyBench evaluates the reference-based checkers used in RLVR for reasoning \citep{yan2025verifybench}, and \citet{huang2025from} show that rule-based verifiers miss equivalent answers in different formats, producing false negatives that hurt RL more as the policy strengthens, whereas model-based verifiers are statically more accurate but highly susceptible to hacking, a finding that feeds directly into \S\ref{sec:safety}.

The embodied judges of \S\ref{sec:embodied} are acquiring quality instruments of their own. ViSTa composes over 4,000 videos with step-by-step descriptions into hierarchies of increasingly complex sequential tasks, testing whether vision-language models (VLMs) can supervise behavior that cannot be scored from the final state alone; the evaluated models recognized objects well but failed to understand task sequence, with only GPT-4o achieving non-trivial performance \citep{wybitul2024vista}. MotIF-1K, with 653 human and 369 robot demonstrations across 13 task categories, plays the same role for motion, exposing the failure of single-frame success detectors whenever success depends on how a trajectory unfolds rather than where it ends \citep{hwang2024motif}. RoboReward's benchmark half, built from success-heavy robot corpora augmented with calibrated failures, finds that no existing open or proprietary VLM excels across its reward-assignment tasks \citep{lee2026roboreward}. All three grade the judge rather than the receiving policy, so embodied evaluation currently occupies the quality axis alone; no benchmark yet measures whether a motor policy improves when verbal feedback is supplied, a gap \S\ref{subsec:eval-gaps} returns to.

\subsection{Measuring Feedback Utilization}
\label{subsec:eval-utilization}

The deliberative pillar assumes that feedback changes behavior through the context window, within an episode or, when it persists as experiential memory, across them; the learning pillar assumes that it changes the weights themselves (\S\ref{sec:deliberative}, \S\ref{sec:learning}). Utilization benchmarks test these assumptions directly rather than taking them on faith: quality benchmarks score the signal, utilization benchmarks score the receiver. RealCritic makes the shift explicit by grading a critique through the correction it induces rather than through open-loop ratings; under this closed-loop metric, classical LLMs can fall below their own baselines in self-critique and iterative settings \citep{tang2025realcritic}. JETTS evaluates judges in the operational roles they occupy during test-time scaling and finds them competitive with outcome reward models for reranking but consistently worse than PRMs in beam search, with natural-language critiques that are currently ineffective at guiding generators to better responses \citep{zhou2025evaluating}.

Interactive environments treat feedback as a controlled experimental variable. MINT gives an early quantification of the marginal value of verbal feedback in multi-turn problem solving: tools add 1--8\% absolute performance per turn and simulated natural-language feedback adds 2--17\%, while better single-turn performance does not guarantee better multi-turn performance \citep{wang2023mint}. ConvCodeWorld crosses compilation, execution, and verbal feedback of varying expertise into nine reproducible code-repair scenarios; performance varies significantly with feedback type, weaker models given sufficient feedback can exceed the single-turn results of stronger ones, and a static replay variant preserves Spearman rank correlations of 0.82--0.99 with the live environment \citep{han2025convcodeworld}. LLF-Bench, whose formal role \S\ref{sec:framework} discusses, is the purest instrument on the utilization axis: with instructions and post-action language feedback as the only learning signal, improvement across attempts measures feedback uptake directly, and paraphrased, randomized verbalizations rule out the competing explanation that the agent is recognizing a familiar task rather than reading the feedback \citep{cheng2023llfbench}; LMRL Gym is its numeric-reward complement, benchmarking multi-turn reinforcement learning when states and actions are text \citep{abdulhai2023lmrl}. The starkest utilization result comes from Feedback Friction: even when the feedback generator has access to near-complete ground-truth answers, solver models consistently resist incorporating its feedback, and sampling-based mitigations improve matters without reaching target performance \citep{jiang2025feedback}. Utilization, not quality, can be the binding constraint.

Agentic settings extend both axes to trajectories and memory. AgentRewardBench evaluates twelve LLM judges against expert annotations of 1,302 web-agent trajectories: no single judge excels across benchmarks, and the rule-based evaluation common in agent benchmarks underreports success rates \citep{lu2025agentrewardbench}. Plan-RewardBench finds generative reward models, discriminative reward models, and LLM judges all facing substantial challenges on long-horizon, tool-integrated trajectory judgment \citep{wang2026aligning}, echoing the credit-assignment difficulties of \S\ref{sec:credit}, and MemoryAgentBench tests whether accumulated verbal experience is accurately retrieved, used for test-time learning, and selectively forgotten across episodes \citep{hu2025evaluating}, the evaluation counterpart of the experiential memory mechanisms of \S\ref{subsec:memory}.

\subsection{Measurement Gaps}
\label{subsec:eval-gaps}

Four gaps stand out once the families in Table~\ref{tab:benchmarks} are read side by side. First, benchmark validity is itself contested. Accuracy-style scores can decorrelate from the downstream quantity they proxy, as RewardMATH demonstrated for math preference pairs \citep{kim2024evaluating}; the corrective practice of validating a benchmark against best-of-$N$, RLHF, or policy outcomes \citep{frick2024how,malik2025rewardbench,zhou2024rmb} should become standard rather than a distinguishing feature. Second, the judges that anchor much VRL evaluation are unreliable instruments: they carry discoverable biases \citep{ye2024justice,lai2026biasscope}, break preference consistency on hard cases \citep{feng2025are}, and feed leaderboards whose aggregation hides ranking uncertainty \citep{feuer2025when}. Any conclusion that rests on judge-scored evidence inherits these error bars. Third, utilization is systematically under-measured: most benchmarks grade signals in an open loop, yet the closed-loop results of RealCritic, JETTS, and Feedback Friction show that correct feedback frequently fails to improve the receiver \citep{tang2025realcritic,zhou2025evaluating,jiang2025feedback}. Fourth, coverage remains thin exactly where VRL is heading: non-English feedback \citep{son2024mmeval}, personalized preference \citep{ma2026personalized}, long-horizon trajectories \citep{wang2026aligning}, cross-episode memory \citep{hu2025evaluating}, and embodied feedback \citep{wybitul2024vista,hwang2024motif,lee2026roboreward} each have at most a few dedicated benchmarks, and the embodied instruments probe only quality, never utilization. Read together, the families also agree on where measurement is trustworthy and where it is not: quality signals are best calibrated on short, verifiable, English, single-turn tasks, and least reliable on stylistically confounded, personalized, multilingual, long-horizon, or embodied ones, which is precisely the regime in which deployed VRL agents operate.

These gaps motivate a minimal reporting protocol for VRL systems. (1)~Report feedback quality and feedback utilization as separate quantities, since either can bottleneck the other. (2)~Validate any feedback-quality benchmark against a downstream criterion, whether best-of-$N$ selection, policy optimization, or task success, before using it for model or signal selection. (3)~Audit the judge or verifier for consistency and bias before trusting its verdicts as evaluation or reward. (4)~State the signal type and stage being measured, so that results transfer only where the taxonomy of \S\ref{sec:taxonomy} says they should. What this protocol cannot yet supply, standardized utilization metrics, adversarial feedback benchmarks, and utilization measurement for the embodied settings of \S\ref{sec:embodied}, where existing instruments grade only the judge, is taken up in the research agenda of \S\ref{sec:practitioner}.
