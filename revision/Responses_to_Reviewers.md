# Responses to Reviewers' Comments

**Manuscript:** The Rise of Verbal Reinforcement Learning: A Survey (ACL ARR 2026 May, Submission 12040)

We sincerely thank the Area Chair and all four reviewers for their careful reading and constructive feedback. We have revised the manuscript to address every comment. All modifications are highlighted in **red** in the revised manuscript (deletions appear in red strikethrough), and page and line numbers below refer to the **marked, line-numbered version** of the revised manuscript. A companion "Summary of Differences" document reproduces the previous and updated text side by side for the major changes.

---

## Reviewer pqAY

| # | Reviewer comment | Our response |
|---|---|---|
| S | *Strengths: timely topic for the ACL community; unified framework for directions usually discussed separately; intuitive temporal framing; broad coverage; goes beyond listing prior work and identifies cross-cutting challenges.* | We sincerely thank the reviewer for this generous assessment and for recommending the paper for Findings. We have worked to preserve these strengths while tightening the scope as suggested below. |
| W1 | *The scope of VRL could be more clearly delineated. The paper defines VRL broadly... it also makes the boundary with adjacent paradigms somewhat unclear... A more concrete discussion of what does not count as VRL, and how VRL differs from RLHF, instruction following, language-conditioned RL, and general LLM-agent feedback loops, would make the framing more precise.* | We fully agree, and we apologize for the imprecision. This comment is addressed as follows: the broad definition is replaced by two explicit inclusion criteria, (i) the linguistic structure of the feedback must be functionally consumed rather than collapsed to a scalar or preference bit before use, and (ii) the feedback must participate in an improvement loop (Section 1, page 2, lines 74–88). The same passage now states what does **not** count as VRL: plain instruction following (no improvement loop), vanilla RLHF/DPO (judgment reduced to a preference bit before learning; retained only as the scalar endpoint of the compression spectrum, see also Section 5.4, page 8, lines 637–641), and language-conditioned RL (language as policy input, not feedback). A boundary clarification for specification-time consumption appears at page 2, lines 92–97, and the abstract now announces the criteria (page 1, lines 7–10). |
| W2 | *The taxonomy would benefit from clearer rules for methods that span multiple pillars... Section 3.4 places reward-code generation under grounding... Section 4.4 treats experiential memory as inference-time deliberative feedback, but memory persists across episodes... explicit decision rules for such hybrid cases, or acknowledge these overlaps more systematically.* | We agree; this was underspecified. This comment is addressed as follows: Section 2 now states an explicit assignment rule, "a method is placed by what its linguistic signal modifies at the moment the signal takes effect" (page 4, lines 239–244), and a new hybrid-cases table (Table 2, page 4, lines 249–252) records primary placements together with secondary roles instead of forcing single-cell membership. For the two cases the reviewer names: reward-code generation now carries the specification-time rationale and an explicit pointer to its downstream training role (Section 3.4, page 5, lines 379–384), and experiential memory is now classified by its moment of consumption with the cross-episode boundary recorded (Section 4.4, page 7, lines 480–485). |
| C1 | *Please consider making terminology more consistent. For example, the paper appears to use both "grounded signal" and "grounding signal"; using one term throughout would improve clarity.* | Thank you for catching this; we apologize for the inconsistency. We have standardized on "grounding signal" and audited the full source: the revised text contains no remaining occurrence of "grounded signal". We are additionally re-exporting the figures to ensure the embedded labels match the standardized term. |
| L1 | *The societal impact discussion could be more explicit. Since VRL agents may act on natural-language feedback from humans, tools, or other models, it would be useful to discuss who may be harmed when feedback is incorrect, biased, adversarial, or poorly grounded... impacts on high-stakes agent applications such as coding assistants, robotics, clinical decision support, and education.* | We agree and thank the reviewer for the concrete framing. This comment is addressed in the expanded Limitations section (page 10, lines 827–832), which now names the failure modes (incorrect, biased, adversarial, or poorly grounded feedback) and discusses who is harmed in the high-stakes deployments the reviewer lists, complementing the existing Section 6 discussion of prompt injection, memory poisoning, and sycophancy. |

## Reviewer Q27s

| # | Reviewer comment | Our response |
|---|---|---|
| S | *Strengths: the topic is timely and useful; the paper collects many relevant strands of work; I like the motivation for a survey in this area.* | We sincerely thank the reviewer for finding the direction exciting and for the unusually actionable critique; the revision below follows it closely. |
| W1 | *The definition of VRL is too broad. The paper defines VRL so broadly that many standard training paradigms could fit, including supervised fine-tuning, RLHF, DPO, and preference optimization. This makes the scope hard to distinguish from general LLM training/alignment.* | We agree, and we are sorry the original framing invited this reading. This comment is addressed as follows: the definition is replaced by two inclusion criteria (Section 1, page 2, lines 74–88) under which supervised fine-tuning on instructions and vanilla RLHF/DPO fall **outside** VRL; they are retained only as the scalar endpoint of the compression spectrum, stated explicitly where preference shaping is discussed (Section 5.4, page 8, lines 637–641). This materially narrows the scope and separates the survey from general LLM training and alignment. |
| W2 | *The term VRL is already known in a narrower sense... introduced for self-reflective agents, but then adopts it much more broadly. This may confuse readers unless the authors more clearly justify the terminology and distinguish their usage.* | We agree. This comment is addressed by an explicit genealogy statement: Section 1 now says that we deliberately generalize the term introduced by Shinn et al. (2023) for self-reflective agents, and states the generalization up front to avoid conflation with the original narrower sense (page 2, lines 110–112). |
| W3 | *The taxonomy feels forced. The three-pillar loop of grounding, feedback, and learning is intuitive as a conceptual picture, but no real method operates in this clean lifecycle. Presenting such a loop can misrepresent how verbal feedback actually works.* | We accept this criticism; the loop framing was misleading. This comment is addressed as follows: the revision removes the lifecycle implication and states that "the pillars classify individual signals, not stages of a pipeline: no method is claimed to traverse a grounding-deliberation-learning cycle" (Section 2, page 4, lines 254–259). The coding-agent example is reframed accordingly, as a system in which signals of all three kinds coexist. |
| W4 | *The survey should be more scoped... the paper effectively tries to cover most of modern LLM agent training and alignment. This breadth causes important omissions and makes the taxonomy less useful. A narrower focus, e.g., verbal feedback without model training, would make the contribution tighter.* | We agree that scoping was the core problem, and the new inclusion criteria (see W1) remove everything scalar-only from scope. We considered the reviewer's specific suggestion of excluding model training entirely; we respectfully kept training-time methods that retain linguistic structure (feedback-conditioned training, critique-based distillation), because excluding them would sever the inference-time and training-time uses of the same signal. We hope the explicit fence, stated exclusions, and the boundary statement in Section 5.4 achieve the intended tightening. |
| W5 | *The MDP framing is limiting... works for some agentic/RL settings, but not all language-agent or feedback-based methods naturally fit an MDP formulation especially considering the need for transition and reward functions.* | We agree in part, and we have clarified our intent. This comment is addressed by an explicit caveat in Section 2: MDP vocabulary serves as an organizing lens rather than a claim that every method admits an MDP formulation; many settings are partially observable, non-Markovian, or lack explicit transition and reward functions (page 4, lines 244–249). |
| W6 | *Search-guided deliberation does not clearly correspond to a feedback category in the same way as self-critique. It seems more like a decoding/search procedure than a distinct verbal-feedback mechanism.* | This is a fair point and we have revised accordingly. The subsection is renamed "Evaluator Feedback in Search" and now opens with the inclusion criterion: the verbal evaluator signal consumed during search is what qualifies, not the search procedure itself, and decoding strategies with no linguistic feedback fall outside VRL (Section 4.5, page 7, lines 502–505). |
| W7 | *The paper would benefit from more synthesis or benchmarking. As written, the paper mostly describes methods. A benchmark, comparison framework, or more concrete analysis of when different verbal-feedback methods work would make the survey more valuable.* | We agree. This comment is addressed in three places: (a) a new evaluation-practice discussion recommending that feedback quality and feedback utilization be reported separately, with the relevant benchmarks (Section 6.2, page 9, lines 692–697); (b) a new practitioner decision table stating when each verbal-feedback mechanism is the right choice (Appendix B, Table 4, page 19); and (c) a named research agenda (Section 6.5, page 10, within lines 775–828). Constructing a new benchmark is beyond a survey's scope, but we believe the comparison framework and decision guidance directly address the comparability gap. |
| C1 | *Reconsider whether "VRL" should be used this broadly.* | Addressed together with W1 and W2: the term is retained but its scope is now fenced by the two criteria, and the genealogy statement distinguishes our generalized usage from the original coinage. |
| C2 | *Narrow the scope or clearly state what is excluded.* | Addressed together with W1 and W4: exclusions are now stated explicitly in Section 1 (page 2, lines 83–88), including instruction following, vanilla RLHF/DPO, and language-conditioned RL. |
